# CREATIVE BIBLE
## Anamorphic Commercial Pipeline — Master Reference

All agents in this pipeline read this document. It defines the aesthetic
language, emotional grammar, structural vocabulary, director references,
and collision taxonomy that govern every treatment, prompt, and evaluation
produced by this system.

---

## 1. THE CORE THEME

The subject of every commercial in this pipeline is **transience** —
the unrepeatable encounter. Two lives that briefly become one thing,
then separate. The beauty is *in* the brevity. Nobody reunites.
Nobody waits by a phone. The moment was the whole story.

This is not a romance pipeline. It is a pipeline about the private
singular moments that exist inside the indifferent city — and the fact
that nobody outside the frame knows they happened.

---

## 2. VISUAL LANGUAGE — ANAMORPHIC SPECIFICATION

Every image produced by this pipeline must conform to these anamorphic
characteristics. These are non-negotiable across all shots in all
treatments.

### Lens Character
- **Aspect ratio**: 2.39:1 letterbox — always
- **Bokeh shape**: oval / elliptical, never circular
- **Lens flares**: horizontal streak only, subtle, motivated by
  practical light sources — never decorative
- **Chromatic aberration**: present at frame edges, slight color
  fringing on high-contrast boundaries
- **Barrel distortion**: mild at extreme edges, more pronounced in
  wider shots
- **Depth of field**: shallow to very shallow — the world beyond the
  subject dissolves

### Image Quality
- **Film grain**: present, consistent — medium grain, not heavy
- **Sharpness**: subject sharp, background painterly
- **Dynamic range**: high — deep shadows retain detail, highlights
  don't blow out
- **Texture**: images should feel physical, not digital

### Color Grade
- **Default palette**: desaturated overall, teal-amber split —
  cool shadows, warm highlights
- **Skin tones**: protected — never green-shifted
- **Black level**: lifted slightly — not crushed
- **Saturation**: intentional color pops allowed (a red coat, a yellow
  light) against desaturated surroundings

### Lighting
- **Always motivated** — every light source has a real-world
  explanation
- **Quality over quantity** — one strong key light beats three soft
  ones
- **Contrast ratio**: high — commercials in this pipeline live in
  shadow as much as light
- **Practical lights**: encouraged — neon, candles, windows, screens,
  street lights create the world

## 3. EMOTIONAL GRAMMAR

These are the rules that govern how a story feels, not just what
it shows.

### What every treatment must have
- **One image that stops you** — the pivot shot. If this image
  doesn't work, the sequence doesn't work.
- **Something withheld** — the audience should feel they are
  missing part of the story. They are meant to feel this.
- **The ordinary inside the extraordinary** — or vice versa.
  The Zoom Out principle: the private singular thing exists
  inside the indifferent continuing world.
- **No explanation** — the commercial does not tell you what
  to feel. It creates conditions for feeling.

### What every treatment must avoid
- Dialogue that explains the emotion
- Close-ups of tears
- Music that tells you this is sad / happy / romantic
- Neat resolution
- Product placement that interrupts the world

### The Glazer Test
Ask of every shot: *would Jonathan Glazer cut this?*
If the answer is "he'd find it too obvious," find the less
obvious version of the same feeling.

## 4. STRUCTURE LIBRARY

The Director agent selects one structure per treatment. Structure
is a variable, not a rule. The same story can be told in multiple
structures — each produces a different emotional experience.

**H (The Zoom Out)** is not a standalone structure. It is a camera
grammar tool that can augment the opening or closing of any
other structure.

### A — Reverse
The commercial begins at the end of the story and walks backward
to the beginning. The audience watches the cause arrive after
seeing the effect. The most joyful shot lands as the most
heartbreaking because of what the viewer already knows.

*Emotional payload*: grief, dramatic irony, the beauty of
what was about to be lost

*Signature move*: the first meeting is the last shot

---

### B — Forward / Collision
Classical structure, executed with restraint. The world is
established. Two things move toward each other without knowing.
They meet. Something is revealed by the meeting that reframes
what we thought we were watching.

*Emotional payload*: fate, the city as machine, surprise

*Signature move*: the reveal changes the meaning of the
opening shot

---

### C — The Loop
The commercial opens and closes on the same image or near-
identical frame. Everything between has changed what the image
means. The audience thinks they've returned to the start —
and realizes the world is different now.

*Emotional payload*: time, circularity, the weight of
the invisible

*Signature move*: the closing frame holds two seconds
longer than the opening

---

### D — The False Lead
We follow the wrong character. The commercial appears to be
about one person — their movement, their world, their feeling.
The real subject is in the background, or arrives at the end.
The pivot is a shift of attention, not a plot twist.

*Emotional payload*: perspective, the invisible person,
who we choose to notice

*Signature move*: the real subject is visible but
unattended in the first shot

---

### E — The Interrupt
A single perfect moment, building — then the commercial cuts
before it completes. We never see the ending. The audience
finishes it in their imagination. What the frame withholds
is more powerful than what it shows.

*Emotional payload*: longing, suspension, the imagination
filling the withheld

*Signature move*: the cut comes one beat earlier than
the audience expects

---

### F — Two Solitudes
No meeting. Two people, same location at different times —
or different locations with rhyming moments. The whole film
is about the gap between them. The audience watches both
threads knowing what neither character knows.

*Emotional payload*: parallel lives, near-misses, the
vastness of what doesn't happen

*Signature move*: both characters perform the same small
action at different moments, unaware of each other

---

### G — The Chain
One action causes everything. A butterfly effect — a small
thing sets a cascade of consequences in motion. Each cut is
a new cause-effect link. The ending recontextualizes the
opening action, making something trivial feel enormous.

*Emotional payload*: fate as mechanics, the weight of
small decisions, dark comedy possible

*Signature move*: the last shot rhymes with the first
in a way that makes the chain feel inevitable

---

### H — The Zoom Out (camera grammar tool)
Not a standalone structure. A way of opening or closing any
treatment. The camera begins inside something — a moment, a
texture, a face — and pulls back through physical space,
through architecture, through rooms, through a city.

The reveal at the end is **ordinary**. That's the point.
The extraordinary private thing exists inside the
indifferent continuing world.

*Use as*: opening (zoom in to find the moment — Jeunet/
fate grammar) or closing (zoom out to reveal the world
that doesn't know — Glazer/indifference grammar)

---

### I — Dilated Time
The entire commercial exists inside one second of real time.
Extreme slow motion, or a frozen-world effect where
everything is held except one thing moving through it.

The emotional logic: this moment is so significant that
time itself has expanded to contain it. The viewer
understands they are inside the subjective experience of
the person in the moment.

*Emotional payload*: the weight of a single second,
time as subjective, the moment that changes everything

*Signature move*: sound design carries the film —
music slows, ambient sound becomes the texture

---

### J — Tableau Vivant (Frozen World)
The world is stopped. The camera is the only thing moving.
The camera travels *through* a frozen moment — between rooms,
between people, between causes and effects. The audience
assembles the story from the evidence the camera reveals.

Something at the end recontextualizes everything. The camera
is the detective. The frozen world is the crime scene.

Reference: *Carousel* (Philips Cinema 21:9, 2009) —
camera moves through a frozen clown heist across multiple
rooms, reveals at the end that the police officer on the
car was one of the criminals all along.

*Emotional payload*: the viewer as god / detective,
the world as evidence, the twist that was always visible

*Signature move*: the reveal was present in the first
shot — the audience simply didn't know to look for it

## 5. COLLISION TAXONOMY

The collision is the moment two things enter the same frame
and change each other. Every treatment requires one collision
type. In some structures (F, I, J) the collision may be
implied rather than shown.

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Bodies in space — a touch, a near-miss, someone stopping someone else's movement |
| **Audible** | Sound crosses the gap — music from one person reaches another, a voice, ambient sound that connects two strangers |
| **Visual** | Eye contact across space, a reflection that contains both people, one person seeing another through glass |
| **Object** | A thing passes between them — dropped, thrown, exchanged, left behind |
| **Environmental** | The world forces contact — rain, a crowd, a closing door, a departing train |
| **Temporal** | They occupy the same space at different times — one leaves something the other finds |
| **Implied** | The collision is never shown — only its aftermath or its approach |

## 6. DIRECTOR REFERENCE LIBRARY

These are the tonal registers available to the Director agent.
Each represents a distinct emotional grammar, not just a visual
style. The Director selects one or declares a hybrid.

### GLAZER
*Dread and beauty in the same frame.*
The viewer is unsettled before they understand why. Bodies
in motion. Music as architecture. Something primal underneath
the surface. The commercial knows something the viewer doesn't,
and it's not going to tell them.

Commercial references: Levi's *Swimmer*, Guinness *Surfers*,
Massive Attack *Karmacoma*

*Use when*: the treatment has an undercurrent of something
wrong, or the beauty needs an edge

---

### JEUNET
*Fate as whimsy. The world is a machine that makes love happen.*
Everything is slightly too beautiful to be real. Movement,
trains, strangers, romantic inevitability. The city is
complicit in the encounter. Magic realism applied to desire.

Commercial references: Chanel No.5 with Audrey Tautou

*Use when*: the treatment is about the city as fate-machine,
or the encounter has an element of the miraculous

---

### ZACHARIÁŠ
*Wit and cool. European youth. Light irony.*
The commercial knows it's a commercial and carries that
self-awareness without breaking. Cultural collision,
linguistic play, young masculine or feminine energy.
The emotion is real but worn lightly.

Commercial references: Levi's *French Dictionary*
with Gael García Bernal

*Use when*: the treatment has a playful register, or
the collision has a wit component

---

### WONG KAR-WAI
*Time, longing, color as emotion.*
In the Mood for Love compressed to sixty seconds. Every
frame is already a still photograph. The camera lingers
on what's almost happening. Slow motion not as effect
but as the natural pace of feeling.

Commercial references: Philips short film, various
fashion campaigns

*Use when*: the treatment is primarily about longing,
or the color grade is doing heavy narrative work

---

### SPIKE JONZE
*Anarchic tenderness. Something absurd that makes you cry.*
The emotion sneaks up through strangeness. Kinetic energy
that resolves into something unexpectedly quiet. The trick
is always the same: make the audience laugh, then make
them feel something they didn't consent to.

Commercial references: Ikea *Lamp* (Cannes Grand Prix),
Kenzo World, Gap

*Use when*: the treatment has a physical comedy or
absurdist element that resolves into genuine feeling

---

### MICHEL GONDRY
*Ingenious mechanics. The how of filmmaking as payload.*
In-camera tricks, loops, physical impossibilities made
real without CGI. The audience's pleasure in the *how*
is part of the emotional experience. Whimsy grounded
in craft.

Commercial references: Smirnoff vodka (invented
bullet-time), various Björk music videos

*Use when*: the treatment has a formal trick — a loop,
a reversal, a physical impossibility — as its core device

---

### SOFIA COPPOLA
*Intimacy, stillness, the female interior life.*
What's not said is louder than what is. The camera
spends time in the space between actions. Beautiful
surfaces that contain private anguish or private joy.
The commercial trusts the audience to read stillness.

Commercial references: Miss Dior, various fashion campaigns

*Use when*: the treatment centers a woman's interior
experience, or the key emotion is private and unexpressed

---

### LACHAPELLE
*Hyper-saturated theatrical maximalism.*
Color as excess. Beauty as provocation. Staged like a
painting, shot like a film. The world is too much — and
that excess is the point. Every frame is a tableau.

Commercial references: Armani Jeans (Ryan Phillippe),
various fashion and music video work

*Use when*: the treatment calls for the image to be
overwhelming rather than restrained — when subtlety
is the wrong register

---

### MARK ROMANEK
*Precision, cold beauty, clinical distance.*
Images of great formal beauty that maintain emotional
remove. The camera observes without consoling. The
effect is of watching something private from outside
a window — the distance is what makes it affecting.

Commercial references: various fashion and luxury campaigns

*Use when*: the treatment benefits from emotional
restraint, or the observation angle is important

## 7. CHARACTER PHILOSOPHY

Characters in this pipeline are **vessels for the theme**, not
protagonists with backstory. The audience should not know their
names, jobs, or histories. What they know is how the character
moves through the world — their physical quality, their
relationship to the location, and the single emotional note
they're carrying when we meet them.

### Character brief format
When the Director generates a character, the brief contains:
- Physical quality (not description — quality: sharp, soft,
  weighted, effortless)
- Relationship to location (belongs here / alien to it /
  passing through)
- Emotional note at opening (what they're carrying)
- One physical detail that recurs (a coat, a way of holding
  something, a gesture)

### Character consistency across a sequence
When generating images, character consistency is maintained
through:
- Reference images fed into Nano Banana Pro (up to 4 characters)
- Identical character brief language in every shot prompt
- Consistent costume, hair, and physical detail descriptions

### When no character is specified
The Director generates a character from the emotional register
of the treatment. The character serves the theme. The theme
is always transience.

## 8. LOCATION PHILOSOPHY

Locations in this pipeline are **emotional environments**, not
settings. The location is always complicit in what happens inside it.

### What makes a good location
- Has its own light — practical sources that create the grade naturally
- Has depth — foreground, midground, background that the anamorphic
  lens can separate
- Has texture — surfaces that catch and hold light
- Is slightly wrong — too empty, too late, too early, the wrong
  season, the wrong hour
- The ordinary made strange by time of day or circumstance

### Location taxonomy
- **Transit** (stations, airports, platforms, escalators) —
  everyone is between things; nobody belongs
- **Margin** (late-night streets, empty markets, closed cafes) —
  the city in its private hours
- **Interior** (apartments, hotel rooms, stairwells) —
  private space with public light bleeding in
- **Threshold** (doorways, windows, the edge of a crowd) —
  between inside and outside, between knowing and not knowing
- **Water** (harbors, bridges, rain-wet streets, fountains) —
  reflection doubles every frame

## 9. THE PIVOT SHOT

Every treatment is built around one image. This is the shot
the entire commercial exists to justify. It is the image that
stops someone scrolling. It is the image the LoRA is being
trained to produce.

### What the pivot shot must do
- Work as a standalone image with no context
- Contain the emotional core of the entire treatment in one frame
- Be visually specific — not a generic beautiful image but *this*
  image, in *this* light, at *this* moment
- Be anamorphically correct in every detail

### What the pivot shot must not do
- Explain itself
- Reproduce a cliché (the kiss, the sunset, the tear)
- Require the surrounding shots to be understood

### The pivot shot as training data
High-scoring pivot shots are the primary targets for LoRA
training. The evaluation pipeline scores pivot shots separately
from sequence shots. A pivot shot that scores 4.5+ on all
axes is added to the training set regardless of the
sequence score.

## 10. THE PRODUCT QUESTION

Not all treatments in this pipeline are for a specific product.
The pipeline supports four modes:

| Mode | Description |
|---|---|
| **Pure** | No product. The commercial is a short film. |
| **Ghost** | A product exists but appears only once, briefly, without interrupting the world. |
| **Integrated** | The product is part of the world — a character uses it the way they would use anything. |
| **Assigned** | A real brand or product is specified as input. The treatment is built around it. |

In all modes, the product never explains the emotion.
The emotion is always earned before the product appears.
